How We Migrated 12 Counties Without Downtime

Migrating a court system that handles thousands of cases daily to a new platform without a single minute of downtime. The strategy, the tooling, and the lessons that apply to any large-scale migration.

In early 2024, we were staring at a spreadsheet with twelve county names, each running a different version of the same aging case management system. Some were on-premise. Two had been "customized" by local IT shops to the point where they barely resembled the original product. All of them needed to move to CaseHub, our modern case management platform, without losing a single filing or missing a single court date.

The constraint was non-negotiable: zero downtime. Courts don't get to close for maintenance. When a judge needs a case file at 9:00 AM Monday, the system has to be there. When an attorney files a motion at 11:47 PM on a Friday, it has to go through. There's no "we'll be back shortly" banner in the justice system.

This is the story of how we pulled it off, and what we learned along the way that applies to any organization facing a large-scale platform migration.

The Parallel Running Strategy

The instinct with migrations is to pick a cutover date, flip the switch, and pray. We've seen that approach fail too many times. Instead, we ran both systems simultaneously for every county. The legacy system remained the system of record while CaseHub operated alongside it, receiving the same data in real time.

We built a synchronization layer that captured every transaction in the legacy system and replayed it in CaseHub. New filings, case updates, hearing schedules, fee payments -- everything was mirrored. This gave us two things: a CaseHub instance that was always current with real production data, and a way to verify that CaseHub handled every transaction correctly before we trusted it as primary.

The parallel period lasted four weeks per county. During that time, clerks and staff could access both systems. They started using CaseHub for read-only lookups first, then gradually for data entry on non-critical workflows. By the time we designated CaseHub as the system of record, the staff had already been using it daily for weeks. The "cutover" was more like turning off the old system than turning on the new one.

Phased Rollout: Small Counties First

We didn't roll out to all twelve counties at once. We started with the three smallest -- counties processing fewer than 200 cases per week. This was deliberate. Smaller volume meant faster validation cycles, shorter parallel running periods, and lower stakes if something went sideways.

Those first three counties surfaced issues we hadn't anticipated. One county had a custom fee schedule with 47 exception rules that existed nowhere in their documentation -- only in the heads of two senior clerks. Another had case numbering conventions that collided with a different county's format. A third had been using a free-text field to store structured data about juvenile cases, which meant we needed a custom parser to extract and properly categorize 11 years of case records.

Every one of those issues would have been catastrophic at scale. Finding them in a 150-case-per-week county meant we could fix them in days. Finding them in the 2,000-case-per-week county would have meant weeks of remediation under pressure.

Data Validation at Every Layer

Migration projects fail most often on data integrity. The code works, the UI looks right, but somewhere in the move, 3% of records got corrupted or lost a field value. In a court system, that 3% represents real cases with real consequences. A missing hearing date means a missed appearance. A corrupted defendant record means the wrong person gets a bench warrant.

We built a three-tier validation system. The first tier ran during synchronization: every record written to CaseHub was immediately read back and compared field-by-field against the source. The second tier ran nightly: a batch process compared aggregate counts and checksums between systems. Total cases by type, total fees collected, total hearings scheduled -- if any number diverged by even one, it triggered an alert.

The third tier was human. We hired temporary staff -- mostly paralegal students -- to manually spot-check 5% of migrated records each week. They pulled random cases from both systems side by side and verified that every field, every document attachment, every hearing note matched. This caught edge cases that automated validation missed: formatting differences that were technically correct but would confuse clerks, date timezone issues that shifted hearings by a day in the display layer, and document attachments that migrated successfully but rendered differently in the new viewer.

The Tooling That Made It Possible

We built three internal tools specifically for this migration. The first was a migration dashboard that showed real-time sync status for every county: records processed, records pending, validation errors, and a health score that aggregated everything into a single number. When that number dropped below 99.7%, someone got paged.

The second tool was a conflict resolver. When the sync layer encountered a record it couldn't automatically migrate -- because of a data type mismatch, a missing reference, or a business rule violation in the new system -- it queued the record for manual review. A clerk-facing interface showed the original record, the attempted migration, and the specific issue. The clerk could fix it, approve it with a note, or escalate it. At peak, we were processing about 40 conflict records per day across all twelve counties. By the end, it was fewer than two.

The third tool was a rollback system. For every county, we maintained the ability to revert to the legacy system within 15 minutes. Not "restore from backup" -- a live, kept-current legacy instance that could become primary again with a DNS change and a configuration flip. We never had to use it. But the fact that we could meant the decision to go live in each county wasn't a leap of faith. It was a calculated step with a safety net.

Lessons That Apply Beyond Court Systems

This was a court system migration, but the principles hold for any organization replacing a critical system. First: parallel running eliminates the "big bang" risk. Yes, it costs more in infrastructure and engineering time. But it converts a single high-risk moment into a gradual, observable transition. The ROI on that reduced risk is enormous.

Second: start small and sequence by complexity, not by politics. The natural impulse is to migrate the most important stakeholder first. Resist it. Migrate the simplest environment first, learn from it, and bring those lessons to the complex ones. Your biggest county -- or your biggest department, or your highest-revenue division -- gets the benefit of every fix you made along the way.

Third: invest in validation tooling early. Every hour spent building automated comparison tools saves ten hours of manual verification and a hundred hours of post-migration fire drills. Data integrity isn't something you check at the end. It's something you monitor continuously from day one.

Finally: build the rollback before you build the migration. Knowing you can go back changes the entire psychology of the project. Teams make better decisions when they're not afraid. Stakeholders approve go-live dates more readily when they know it's reversible. And if something genuinely goes wrong -- not a minor data issue, but a systemic failure -- you have fifteen minutes to normal instead of fifteen days.

Twelve counties. 87,000 active cases migrated. 340,000 historical records transferred. Four months from first county go-live to last county go-live. Zero downtime. Zero data loss.

Not because we got lucky. Because we planned for every way it could go wrong, and built the systems to catch problems before they became crises.
