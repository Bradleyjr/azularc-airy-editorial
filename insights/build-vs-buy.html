The Build vs. Buy Decision Framework

After 200+ projects, we've developed a practical framework for when to build custom software and when to buy off-the-shelf. The answer isn't always "build."

We're a custom software shop. We make our living building things from scratch. So you might expect our answer to "build or buy?" to always be "build." It isn't. In fact, one of the most valuable things we do for clients is tell them when they shouldn't hire us -- when an off-the-shelf product will solve their problem faster, cheaper, and better than anything we could build.

But we've also seen the opposite mistake far more often: companies buying packaged software that forces them to reshape their operations around someone else's assumptions. They spend two years implementing, another year customizing, and end up with a system that does 70% of what they need and none of what makes them different. Then they call us.

After 200+ engagements across courts, manufacturers, and growth-stage companies, we've distilled the build vs. buy decision into five criteria that consistently predict the right answer. Not every time -- nothing works every time -- but reliably enough that we use this framework at the start of every project.

Criterion 1: How Unique Is Your Workflow?

This is the single most important factor, and the one most teams get wrong. The question isn't "is our business unique?" -- every business thinks it's unique. The question is: "are the workflows this software needs to support meaningfully different from standard industry practice?"

If you're running accounts payable, your workflow isn't unique. Every AP department does roughly the same thing: receive invoices, match them to POs, approve, pay. Buy QuickBooks or NetSuite or whatever fits your scale. If you're a court clerk processing eFiling, your workflow is governed by your jurisdiction's specific rules, case types, and procedural requirements -- and those differ meaningfully from the next county over. That's a build.

The test we use: Can you configure an off-the-shelf product to match your workflow in under 40 hours? If yes, buy. If the configuration effort starts approaching the build effort, you're paying twice -- once for the license and once for the customization -- and you'll keep paying for every upgrade that breaks your customizations.

Criterion 2: Is This a Competitive Advantage?

Some software is infrastructure. Email, CRM, project management -- these are table stakes. Nobody wins market share because they have a better internal project management tool. Buy the best available option and move on.

But some software is the product, or it directly enables the product. If you're a manufacturer and your 3D product configurator is what lets dealers close sales in the showroom, that's competitive advantage. If you're a court system and your citizen portal is what reduces walk-in traffic by 70%, that's a differentiator. You don't want your competitive advantage running on the same platform your competitors can license next quarter.

We've had this conversation dozens of times with growth-stage companies. They'll say, "Our operations are our moat." Then they'll try to run those operations on Salesforce with custom objects and Apex triggers. If your operations are genuinely your moat, the software that runs them should be purpose-built to protect that advantage, not rented from a vendor who's also selling to your competitors.

Criterion 3: What Are the Integration Requirements?

Every packaged software vendor will tell you their product "integrates with everything." What they mean is: it has an API. Having an API and having a reliable, real-time integration that handles edge cases, error recovery, and data consistency across systems are very different things.

If the software you're evaluating needs to exchange data with one or two other systems in relatively simple ways -- syncing contacts between your CRM and email tool, for example -- buy. The integrations are solved problems and there are probably Zapier templates for them.

If the software needs to be the hub of a complex data ecosystem -- pulling from multiple upstream systems, transforming data, pushing to multiple downstream consumers, handling real-time events -- you're going to hit the limits of packaged integration tools fast. We've seen companies spend more on middleware, iPaaS subscriptions, and integration consultants than they would have spent building a custom system that handles the data flows natively. At a certain integration complexity threshold, custom software isn't the expensive option. It's the only option that actually works.

Criterion 4: Total Cost of Ownership

This is where the math gets interesting, and where most buy decisions go wrong. Companies compare the upfront cost of a custom build against the annual license fee of a packaged product and conclude that buying is cheaper. That comparison ignores about 60% of the actual costs.

For a packaged product, total cost includes: license fees (which increase annually), implementation consulting, customization and configuration, training, per-seat costs as you grow, integration development and maintenance, the productivity cost of workflows that don't quite fit, and the opportunity cost of features the vendor won't build. Add those up over five years and the number is often three to five times the initial license quote.

For a custom build, total cost includes: development, infrastructure, ongoing maintenance, and future feature development. The upfront number is higher. The five-year number is often lower. And critically, the trajectory is different: packaged software costs tend to increase every year as vendors raise prices and you add seats. Custom software maintenance costs tend to decrease after the first year as the system stabilizes.

"The most expensive software decision isn't building something you should have bought. It's buying something that almost works and spending the next five years trying to close the gap."

Criterion 5: Maintenance and Evolution

This is the criterion that tips the scales most often, and it's the one teams think about least. Software isn't a one-time purchase. It's a living system that needs to evolve as your business evolves. The question is: who controls that evolution?

With a packaged product, the vendor controls the roadmap. You can request features, vote on them, even pay for priority development. But ultimately, the vendor is building for their entire customer base, not for you. If your needs diverge from the mainstream -- and in our experience, they always do eventually -- you're either waiting for the vendor to catch up or building workarounds yourself.

With a custom build, you control the roadmap. Every dollar of development budget goes toward your priorities. The tradeoff is that you need a plan for ongoing maintenance: security patches, infrastructure updates, dependency management. This isn't free, but it's predictable, and it's yours to control. We typically budget 15-20% of the original build cost annually for maintenance and incremental improvements. Most of our clients find that's less than their annual vendor price increases were.

The Decision Matrix

Here's the simplified version we use in initial conversations. Score each criterion from 1 to 5, where 1 strongly favors buying and 5 strongly favors building:

Criterion Buy Signal (1-2) Build Signal (4-5)
Workflow Uniqueness Standard industry process Jurisdiction or domain-specific rules
Competitive Advantage Internal operations / table stakes Core differentiator or revenue driver
Integration Complexity 1-2 simple data syncs Hub of a multi-system ecosystem
5-Year TCO License + config < custom build License + customization > custom build
Evolution Control Vendor roadmap aligns with your needs Needs will diverge from mainstream

Total score 5-12: Strong buy signal. 13-17: Depends on context. 18-25: Strong build signal.

The framework isn't meant to give you a definitive answer from a spreadsheet. It's meant to structure the conversation so you're evaluating the right factors instead of defaulting to whichever option feels less risky in the moment.

In our experience, most companies that score in the 13-17 range end up building -- but building something smaller than they initially planned. They buy for the commodity workflows and build for the parts that make them different. That hybrid approach works well when it's intentional. It's a disaster when it happens accidentally, which is what happens when you buy a big platform and then build customizations on top of it.

The best decision is the one you make deliberately, with clear criteria, before you've sat through six vendor demos and started anchoring on a specific product. Run the framework first. Then go shopping -- or go building -- with confidence that you're solving the right problem the right way.
