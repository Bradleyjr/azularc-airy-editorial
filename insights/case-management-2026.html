Case Management in 2026: What Courts Need Now

State mandates are tightening. Public expectations are rising. And most court systems are still running software built before smartphones existed. Here's what modern case management actually requires.

We've been building case management systems for courts for over a decade. In that time, the mandates have changed, the public's expectations have shifted dramatically, and the technology available to solve these problems has leaped forward several generations. But walk into most courthouses in America and you'll find the same software that was installed during the Bush administration. Not the second one. The first one.

This isn't a technology problem. It's an inertia problem. Court administrators know their systems are outdated. Clerks know. Judges know. The citizens who have to interact with these systems definitely know. But replacing a case management system feels enormous -- and when you're processing thousands of active cases, the risk of getting it wrong feels even bigger than the cost of doing nothing.

Except the cost of doing nothing keeps going up. And in 2026, the gap between what courts are running and what they actually need has become untenable.

The Mandate Landscape Has Changed

State legislatures have been steadily tightening requirements around electronic filing, public access, and data reporting. What used to be optional is now mandatory. Courts that could get away with paper-based processes five years ago are now facing compliance deadlines they can't meet with their existing systems.

eFiling mandates are the most visible example, but they're just the start. States are requiring real-time data reporting to central administrative offices. Federal grant programs increasingly require digital case tracking as a condition of funding. ADA compliance standards for public-facing systems have gotten significantly more specific. And privacy regulations around case data -- particularly in family and juvenile cases -- are creating new requirements that legacy systems were never designed to handle.

The courts that have been able to meet these mandates through workarounds -- exporting data manually, running parallel paper processes, hiring temp staff during filing surges -- are running out of workarounds. The mandates are too numerous and too interconnected to patch one at a time.

What eFiling Actually Requires

Most courts that claim to have eFiling have something closer to "email with extra steps." A PDF gets uploaded through a portal, someone on the other end downloads it, manually enters data from the document into the case management system, and files the physical printout. That's not eFiling. That's digital delivery of paper.

Real eFiling means the document submission, data extraction, validation, docketing, and notification happen as a single automated workflow. When an attorney files a motion, the system should parse the document, validate it against the case record, assign it a docket entry, notify all parties of record, calculate any response deadlines based on the applicable rules, and update the judge's calendar -- without a clerk touching it. The clerk's role shifts from data entry to exception handling: reviewing the filings the system flags as incomplete, unusual, or requiring discretionary decisions.

This isn't theoretical. We've built this. Our CaseHub platform processes filings this way across multiple Georgia counties today. The difference in throughput is staggering -- filing processing time dropped by 45% in the first six months, and clerk overtime during peak filing periods essentially disappeared.

Real-Time Notifications and Public Access

Citizens expect to interact with their court system the same way they interact with every other service in their lives: on their phone, on their schedule. They want to check case status without calling the clerk's office. They want hearing reminders sent to their phone. They want to pay fines and fees online at 10 PM on a Sunday.

This isn't an unreasonable expectation. Banks figured this out fifteen years ago. Healthcare figured it out ten years ago. Courts are the last major public institution still requiring people to show up in person or call during business hours for basic information.

Modern case management needs a public-facing portal that's genuinely useful, not a search page that returns cryptic docket codes. Status updates in plain language. Automated SMS and email notifications for hearings, deadlines, and case events. Online payment processing with real-time receipt and account updates. And all of it accessible on a phone screen without zooming and scrolling through a desktop layout shrunk to fit.

The access question goes beyond convenience. Courts that make it difficult for citizens to access case information create a two-tier system: people who can afford attorneys to navigate the bureaucracy, and people who can't. A well-designed public portal is an equity issue as much as an efficiency one.

Role-Based Access and Audit Trails

Legacy systems tend to have two access levels: full access and no access. Maybe a read-only mode if you're lucky. That's not sufficient for a system that handles sealed cases, juvenile records, domestic violence protective orders, and grand jury proceedings alongside routine civil filings.

Modern case management requires granular, role-based permissions. A clerk in the civil division shouldn't see juvenile case data. A judge should see everything in their assigned cases but not be able to modify docket entries directly. An attorney of record should see all filings in their cases but nothing in unrelated cases. Public users should see only what's legally designated as public record, with sealed and restricted documents automatically excluded.

And every single access event needs to be logged. Not just who modified a record, but who viewed it, when, and from where. Audit trails aren't optional anymore -- they're the mechanism courts use to demonstrate compliance, investigate potential breaches, and maintain public trust. When a high-profile case hits the news and someone asks whether anyone accessed the file inappropriately, you need an answer in minutes, not weeks.

"The courts that will thrive in the next decade are the ones that treat their case management system as critical infrastructure -- not as a back-office tool that clerks have to put up with."

The Integration Problem Nobody Talks About

A case management system doesn't exist in isolation. It needs to talk to law enforcement databases for warrant information. It needs to send data to state administrative offices for reporting. It needs to connect to payment processors. It needs to exchange information with other courts when cases transfer jurisdiction. And increasingly, it needs to interface with probation systems, diversion programs, and social services.

Legacy systems handle integrations through batch files and manual exports. Someone runs a report, saves it as a CSV, and uploads it to another system. This happens hundreds of times a day across thousands of courts. It's slow, error-prone, and impossible to audit properly.

Modern case management needs an API-first architecture where integrations happen in real time. When a warrant is served, the case record updates automatically. When a fine is paid, the state reporting system knows immediately. When a case transfers to another county, the receiving court has the complete file before the defendant arrives. This isn't aspirational technology. It's standard practice in every other industry. Courts just haven't demanded it from their vendors -- and most vendors haven't offered it because batch files are cheaper to build.

What Moving Forward Looks Like

The courts we've worked with that have successfully modernized share a few common traits. They started with a clear-eyed assessment of their current systems and processes -- not what the vendor said the system could do, but what it actually did day-to-day. They involved clerks, judges, and attorneys in the design process from the beginning, not just at the end for sign-off. They migrated incrementally, by case type or jurisdiction, rather than trying to switch everything overnight.

And critically, they chose purpose-built solutions over generic platforms. The difference matters. A case management system built specifically for courts understands case types, docket codes, filing rules, hearing calendars, and compliance reporting natively. A generic workflow platform can be configured to approximate these things, but the configuration burden never ends, and the result always feels like a compromise.

2026 is the year where delay stops being a strategy. The mandates are here. The public expectations aren't going back to where they were. And the gap between what legacy systems can do and what courts actually need is only getting wider. The courts that move now will be the ones setting the standard for the next decade. The ones that wait will spend that decade catching up.
