Why Mid-Market Companies Get Stuck on Bad Software

Too big for off-the-shelf. Too small for enterprise vendors. The mid-market software gap is real, and escaping it requires a different approach entirely.

There's a specific kind of frustration we hear from companies in the $20M to $200M revenue range. It sounds like this: "We've outgrown QuickBooks but we're not big enough for SAP." Or: "Salesforce wants $400K per year and a 14-month implementation. The free CRM doesn't do half of what we need. What are we supposed to do?"

What they're describing is the mid-market software gap. It's the space where companies are too complex for tools designed for small businesses but too small to attract the attention (or afford the price tag) of enterprise software vendors. And it's where an enormous amount of productivity goes to die.

We've worked with over 200 companies in this segment across manufacturing, courts, professional services, and growth-stage tech. The pattern is remarkably consistent, and so is the way out.

The gap is structural, not accidental

The software industry is built around two business models. Consumer and SMB tools are designed for volume: low price, self-serve, one-size-fits-all. Enterprise tools are designed for scale: high price, dedicated sales teams, heavy customization. Neither model works well for the middle.

Off-the-shelf SMB software assumes your business looks like every other business in your category. It handles the basics well and completely ignores the things that make your operation unique. You can usually get 70% of what you need out of the box. The remaining 30% is where you spend the next three years building spreadsheet workarounds and begging the vendor for features they'll never build because your segment isn't big enough to justify the engineering time.

Enterprise software, on the other hand, can be configured to do almost anything. But the licensing fees start at six figures, the implementation timelines are measured in years, and the vendor's sales process is designed for Fortune 500 procurement departments, not a VP of operations who needs a decision by next quarter.

So mid-market companies end up in a holding pattern. They duct-tape together a collection of SaaS tools that don't talk to each other, layer on manual processes to fill the gaps, and tell themselves they'll "figure out the software situation" when they're bigger. Except bigger just means more data flowing through the same broken system. The problem compounds. It never resolves on its own.

The three traps that keep companies stuck

Through hundreds of projects, we've identified three patterns that keep mid-market companies on bad software far longer than they should be.

Trap 1: The sunk cost spiral. You've already spent $150K customizing your current platform. You've trained 40 people on it. You've built integrations with your accounting system, your warehouse, your CRM. Walking away from all of that feels like throwing money away. So you spend another $50K on the next round of customizations, which adds to the sunk cost, which makes it even harder to walk away next year. Each patch increases the switching cost, which is exactly what keeps you stuck.

The truth is that sunk costs are gone regardless. The only question that matters is: what's the best path forward from where you are right now? In our experience, once total annual cost of ownership exceeds the cost of replacement divided by two, the math favors building new. Most mid-market companies pass that threshold years before they act on it.

Trap 2: The "we're too unique" belief. Many mid-market companies have convinced themselves that their workflows are so specialized that no software could handle them. This is almost never true. What's actually happening is that their current software forced them into unique workarounds, and those workarounds became the workflow. They're not complex because their business demands it. They're complex because their software created that complexity.

When we do process mapping with new clients, we typically find that 60-70% of their workflows are standard industry patterns. Another 20% are minor variations. Only about 10% require genuinely custom logic. The goal isn't to automate every quirk. It's to handle the standard patterns well and give the team clean tools for the genuinely unique parts.

Trap 3: The fear of the migration. Data migration from legacy systems is legitimately hard. We won't pretend otherwise. But it's a solved problem. We've migrated court systems with millions of active case records. We've moved manufacturers off 15-year-old ERP systems with decades of transaction history. It requires careful planning, good tooling, and a team that's done it before. What it doesn't require is the 18-month timeline and seven-figure budget that enterprise consultants quote.

"We spent two years evaluating enterprise platforms before realizing that what we actually needed was a focused system that did our five core workflows really well. Not a platform that could theoretically do everything."

CEO of a $60M specialty manufacturer, after completing a 4-month custom build

The way out: build for fit, not for features

The mid-market companies that escape the software gap share a common approach. They stop looking for a product that does everything and start building a system that does their specific things exceptionally well.

This doesn't mean building everything from scratch. It means making deliberate choices about what to buy, what to build, and how to connect the pieces. Use Stripe for payments. Use a proven auth provider for security. Use an off-the-shelf accounting system. But build the core workflow engine that runs your actual business, because that's the part no vendor will ever get right for you.

The economics work because you're not paying for 10,000 features you'll never use. You're paying for the 50 features that matter and getting them built exactly the way your team works. The result is software that's cheaper to maintain, faster to evolve, and dramatically easier to use because it was designed for your people, not for a market segment.

Here's what we recommend to mid-market companies ready to make the move:

  • check_circle Start with the pain. Identify the 3-5 workflows that cause the most friction. That's your scope. Not "replace everything" but "fix the things that hurt the most."
  • check_circle Calculate the real cost. Not just licensing fees. Total cost: workarounds, lost productivity, missed opportunities, hiring premium for legacy skills. The real number is what justifies the investment.
  • check_circle Ship in phases. Don't try to replace everything at once. Launch with core workflows, run parallel for 30 days, cut over, then expand. This reduces risk and builds organizational confidence.
  • check_circle Own your data model. Whatever you build, make sure you own the data structure. Vendor lock-in happens at the data layer. If your data is clean and portable, you always have options.

The mid-market software gap isn't going away. If anything, it's widening as enterprise tools get more complex and SMB tools stay basic. But companies that recognize the gap for what it is, a structural problem that requires a structural solution, find that the path forward is shorter and cheaper than they expected. The worst strategy is the one most companies default to: waiting for a product that fits perfectly. That product isn't coming. The better move is to build the thing that actually works for you.
